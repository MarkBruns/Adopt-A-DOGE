# **2024 Cass Review on Gender Identity** 

(full title: Independent Review of Gender Identity Services for Children and Young People) is a comprehensive 2024 independent report commissioned by NHS England and led by Dr. Hilary Cass, a former president of the Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health. 

The **final report** was published on April 10, 2024 (388 pages, plus appendices and separate systematic reviews). It examined the UK's Gender Identity Development Service (GIDS) at Tavistock (now closed) and broader care for children and young people (under 18) experiencing gender-related distress. The review included stakeholder engagement, data analysis, and **eight University of York systematic evidence reviews** on topics like puberty blockers, hormones, social transition, and psychosocial interventions.

## Core Findings on Evidence Quality
The review's central conclusion is that the **evidence base** for medical interventions in youth gender care is **"remarkably weak"** — due to poorly designed studies, short follow-up, high risk of bias, and lack of long-term outcome data. No robust conclusions can be drawn about benefits for gender dysphoria or mental health in most cases. International guidelines (e.g., WPATH, Endocrine Society) were critiqued for low methodological quality, lack of independence, and circular referencing. Psychosocial interventions have evidence "as weak as research on endocrine treatment."

## Puberty Blockers
- Evidence from 50 studies (mostly low/moderate quality) shows **no credible benefits** for mental health or dysphoria reduction.
- Risks include compromised bone density, potential impacts on neurocognitive/psychosexual development, and fertility.
- Nearly 100% of those starting blockers progressed to cross-sex hormones — undermining the "time to think" rationale.
- Early use in birth-registered males can complicate future surgeries due to insufficient genital growth.
- **Conclusion**: Not routinely recommended; only possible in a strict research protocol with narrow indications (e.g., birth-registered males to halt irreversible puberty).

Direct report quote (via summaries): "In summary, there seems to be a very narrow indication for the use of puberty blockers... Other indications remain unproven at this time."

## Cross-Sex Hormones (Masculinising/Feminising)
- Evidence from 53 studies (mostly low/moderate quality): Some short-term psychological improvements (possibly placebo or expectation effects), but insufficient data on physical benefits, long-term mental health, or risks (e.g., cardiovascular, metabolic, reproductive).
- **Conclusion**: Use with **extreme caution** under 18; not before age 16, and only with clear clinical rationale plus multidisciplinary approval. NHS England was advised to review its policy.

## Social Transition
- Evidence from 11 studies (mostly low quality): Insufficient to determine positive or negative mental health effects.
- It is an "**active intervention**" that may influence the trajectory of gender identity development (especially in younger children).
- **Conclusion**: Approach cautiously; not recommended as a default for pre-pubertal children without expert clinical input. Parental involvement is essential.

## Mental Health and Comorbidities
- High rates of co-occurring issues (autism, ADHD, trauma, anxiety, depression, eating disorders) — often more prevalent than in the general population.
- "Diagnostic overshadowing" occurs when gender distress overshadows other needs.
- Most young people will **not** have a persistent trans identity into adulthood; many distress cases resolve with holistic support.
- **Conclusion**: Treat as standard paediatric/mental health cases first. Address underlying issues via local services (e.g., CAMHS) before considering medical pathways.

## Service Model and Broader Issues
- The specialist "affirmative" gender-clinic model (rapid medicalisation) is unsuitable; replace with **holistic, evidence-based paediatric care** integrated into mainstream NHS services.
- Poor governance, lack of data collection/follow-up (adult clinics blocked linkage studies), long waiting lists, and clinician burnout were highlighted.
- Safeguarding risks can be overlooked when focus is solely on gender.
- Rise in referrals (especially adolescent females) needs better understanding; social influences and mental health trends may play roles.

## Main Recommendations (32 total)
- **Holistic care first**: Psycho-social support as default; individualised plans screening for neurodiversity and mental health.
- **Medical restrictions**: Puberty blockers only in research; hormones extremely cautious.
- **New structure**: Regional hubs/networks (ages 17–25 continuity); national multidisciplinary team for approvals; formal training and data infrastructure.
- **Research**: Major programme (including living systematic reviews) to build high-quality evidence on all interventions.
- **Other**: Fertility counselling, detransition support, cautious private care regulation, NHS number change safeguards.

## Immediate Impact
NHS England fully accepted the findings and implemented changes rapidly: routine puberty blockers were withdrawn (policy updated before final report), new regional services launched, and a similar adult review announced. This shifted the UK away from the "gender-affirming" model toward standard evidence-based paediatric care. The report has influenced international debates and policies (e.g., restrictions in several European countries), though it faced criticism from some LGBTQ+ groups and clinicians over methodology and perceived bias. Cass noted widespread online misinformation about its contents.

The full report and York systematic reviews remain the definitive source; they emphasise that care must meet the **same rigorous standards** as any other paediatric treatment. No major revisions to these core findings have emerged as of 2026.
